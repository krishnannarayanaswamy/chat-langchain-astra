 The image is a flowchart that shows the mechanism of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). DIC is a condition in which blood clots form throughout the body, blocking blood flow and causing organ damage.

The flowchart begins with damage to the vascular endothelium, which is the lining of the blood vessels. This damage can be caused by a variety of factors, such as infection, trauma, or surgery.

When the vascular endothelium is damaged, platelets adhere to the injured area and become activated. Activated platelets release a variety of substances that promote coagulation, including thrombin and fibrinogen.

Thrombin converts fibrinogen into fibrin, which forms a mesh that traps red blood cells and platelets, forming a clot. The clot helps to stop bleeding, but it can also block blood flow to organs and tissues.

DIC occurs when the coagulation process is activated too much or when the body's natural anticoagulant mechanisms are overwhelmed. This can lead to the formation of multiple clots throughout the body, which can block blood flow and cause organ damage.

DIC is a serious condition that can be fatal. Treatment for DIC typically involves the use of anticoagulant medications to prevent further clot formation.
