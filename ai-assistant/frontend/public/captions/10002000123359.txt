 Chronic Suppurative Otitis Media (CSOM)

Definition
Chronic suppurative otitis media (CSOM) is a persistent or recurrent infection of the middle ear that lasts for more than 2 weeks. It is characterized by the presence of otorrhea (ear discharge) and may be accompanied by hearing loss, pain, and other symptoms.

Causes
CSOM is most commonly caused by bacteria, such as Streptococcus pneumoniae, Haemophilus influenzae, and Moraxella catarrhalis. These bacteria can enter the middle ear through the Eustachian tube, which is a canal that connects the middle ear to the back of the throat.

Other causes of CSOM include:

- Allergies
- Cleft palate
- Diabetes
- Immunodeficiency disorders
- Nasal polyps
- Sinusitis
- Tobacco smoke exposure

Symptoms
The most common symptom of CSOM is otorrhea. The discharge may be clear, yellow, or green, and may be accompanied by a foul odor.

Other symptoms of CSOM include:

- Hearing loss
- Pain
- Tinnitus (ringing in the ears)
- Vertigo ( dizziness)
- Facial paralysis

Complications
CSOM can lead to a number of complications, including:

- Mastoiditis: This is an infection of the mastoid bone, which is located behind the ear.
