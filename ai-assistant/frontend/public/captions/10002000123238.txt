Muscular dystrophy is a group of inherited, progressive neuromuscular disorders that are manifested in the death or degeneration of skeletal muscles. These disorders are caused by mutations in genes responsible for the production of proteins involved in muscle structure and function. The most common muscular dystrophy is Duchenne muscular dystrophy, which affects 1 in 5,000 men.

Muscular dystrophies are classified into two main types: dystrophinopathies and non-dystrophinopathies. Dystrophinopathies are caused by mutations in the dystrophin gene, which encodes a protein that is essential for the structural integrity of muscle cells. Non-dystrophinopathies are caused by mutations in other genes that are involved in muscle function.

The clinical manifestations of muscular dystrophy vary depending on the type of dystrophy. However, some common symptoms include muscle weakness, muscle wasting, difficulty walking, and respiratory problems.

The diagnosis of muscular dystrophy is based on a combination of clinical findings, laboratory tests, and genetic testing. Laboratory tests may include serum creatine kinase (CK) levels, electromyography (EMG), and muscle biopsy. Genetic testing can identify the specific genetic mutation that is causing the dystrophy.

There is no cure for muscular dystrophy, but there are treatments that can help to slow the progression of the disease and improve symptoms. Treatments include physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy, and medications.

The prognosis for muscular dystrophy varies depending on the type of dystrophy. However, most people with muscular dystrophy will eventually lose the ability to walk and will require assistance with activities of daily living.
