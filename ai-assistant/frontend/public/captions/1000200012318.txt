Sickle-cell anemia is a genetic disorder that affects the shape of red blood cells. Red blood cells are normally round, but in people with sickle-cell anemia, they are elongated and have a crescent shape. These sickle-shaped red blood cells can get stuck in small blood vessels, blocking the flow of blood and oxygen to the body's tissues. This can cause pain, tissue damage, and organ failure.

Symptoms of sickle-cell anemia can vary from person to person. Some people may have only mild symptoms, while others may have severe symptoms that require frequent medical care. Symptoms of sickle-cell anemia can include:

Painful episodes: Painful episodes are the most common symptom of sickle-cell anemia. These episodes can occur when sickle-shaped red blood cells block blood flow to the body's tissues. Painful episodes can last for hours or even days.
Tissue damage: Sickle-shaped red blood cells can damage the tissues of the body, including the heart, brain, lungs, and kidneys. This damage can lead to serious health problems, such as stroke, heart attack, and kidney failure.
Organ failure: Sickle-shaped red blood cells can block blood flow to organs, which can lead to organ failure. Organ failure can be fatal.

Treatment for sickle-cell anemia depends on the severity of the symptoms. Treatment options include:

Pain management: Painful episodes can be treated with pain medication, such as opioids.
Blood transfusions: Blood transfusions can be used to replace sickle-shaped red blood cells with healthy red blood cells.
Hydroxyurea: Hydroxyurea is a medication that can help to prevent sickle-shaped red blood cells from forming.
Stem cell transplant: A stem cell transplant is a procedure that can cure sickle-cell anemia.

Sickle-cell anemia is a serious disease, but it can be managed with treatment. People with sickle-cell anemia can live full and productive lives.
